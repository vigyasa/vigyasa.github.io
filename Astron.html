ASTRON - Shivam Chitnis

While brainstorming for the first project of Vigyasa on my terrace, I glanced at my 8-inch Dobsonian and suddenly the idea struck me. The three of us designed a basic outline and a to-do list for the project, and just like that 'Astron' started.

'Reaching for the stars is all about couple of lenses and no light pollution'

I wanted more people to enjoy stargazing the same way I did. While a lot of hobbies are expensive or require access to equipment not easily available to kids from small towns, astronomy requires nothing but a cloudless night.
The idea with Astron was to introduce interested students to amateur astronomy through video lectures and stargazing sessions. I pitched it to a few schools in my neighbourhood. For the introductory lecture around 60 students turned up.Over the next few weeks, kids of all ages, and in some cases their parents, would gather on school rooftops at 3 or 4 in the morning while I would get my 8 inch Dobsonian telescope for observation.

By 7 in the morning, we would wrap up the stargazing session and then move on to a presentation on a particular topic. Jupiter, Saturn, planetary moons, moon mapping, Sun spots - the stargazing sessions kept getting more and more exciting as I learnt from their questions and they learnt from mine. Kids in the 3rd or 4th grade would come with their own Space encyclopedias, and ask me about nebulae and black holes. One of the smaller kids even asked me, "I understand why planets revolve around the Sun - because of gravity, but why do they rotate around their axes?" I told him I didn't really know, but I'd do the research and get back to him. A lot of teachers and students downloaded their own star maps on smartphones and became adept at recognising the more common constellations. Some of them developed an interest in space flight stories, others found the idea of alien life more interesting.
